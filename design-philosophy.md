# Darel Veterinary Clinic — Design Philosophy

## Movement: **"Natural-History Editorial"**

A visual language for a thirty-year independent veterinary clinic that has, by every account, never had a website worthy of its practice. Darel is one of the oldest premium veterinary clinics in Tashkent — confirmed thirty-plus years of operation, an established reputation as one of the most expensive in the city, a real surgical practice, ultrasound and X-ray on site — and the only digital footprint is a Yellow Pages stub, an Instagram, and an email on `mail.ru`. The brand a generation of pet owners trusts with surgery is invisible online.

The visual reference is not the cheerful cartoon-vet template used by chain animal hospitals worldwide — pastel paws, primary-colour mascots, a smiling dog in the hero. The reference is a small natural-history-museum publication crossed with a modern Scandinavian veterinary practice. The kind of printed object you find on the side table of a quiet examining room: heavy paper, warm sepia ink, anatomical-illustration restraint, a single muted accent of brass-amber, and a discipline of whitespace that says *this is a place where things are done carefully*.

Form is editorial-clinical without being sterile. The page is built around the kind of facts a serious pet owner actually wants before bringing a six-year-old cat in for a soft-tissue ultrasound: who is the head veterinarian, what training do they hold, what equipment is on site, what does a consultation cost, and — critically — *can I bring my dog on a Saturday afternoon when she suddenly stops eating*. Every section answers one of those questions in the form a thoughtful person would expect to read it: a service catalogue with prices in Atkinson Hyperlegible tabular figures, a chief-veterinarian biography under a portrait, an equipment list beside the year of acquisition, an honest hours table that confronts the weekday-only-issue head-on with the new weekend hours.

Colour is the palette of a natural-history monograph and nothing else. **Bone** (#ede4d3) is the page — the warm off-white of a museum publication, not the cold white of a hospital. **Sepia** (#281e14), with the warm dark of pressed walnut, is the primary text — almost black, but never the cold true black of a medical brochure. **Deep petrol** (#1f3a40) is the brand colour — a muted teal-blue distinct from the clinical-blue of every chain veterinary site, distinct from the indigo and forest already used in this portfolio, the colour of a vet's working coat after a decade. **Honey-amber** (#c89548) is the single warm accent, used like a curator uses a label tag — once a section, where the eye should land on a price or a head-vet's name. **Walnut** and **mauve-clay** appear once or twice as deeper tonal supports. No bright red, no friendly orange, no decorative pet-print pattern.

Typography pairs three voices, none used in any of the previous seven commissions in this portfolio. **EB Garamond** is the display voice — Renaissance old-style serif, the typeface of academic veterinary publishing for half a century, with the gravitas of a thirty-year practice. **Atkinson Hyperlegible** is the body voice — designed by the Braille Institute specifically for legibility under difficult conditions; for a clinic whose audience includes elderly pet owners and tearful families reading at midnight, this is not a stylistic choice, it is a duty-of-care choice. **DM Mono** is reserved for phone numbers, prices, opening hours and equipment manufacturer codes — the technical truths of a clinic, set in tabular figures the way a bill of services is set.

Motion is governed by the ui-ux-pro-max rules: **150–300 ms** on micro-interactions, **transform/opacity only** (per `transform-performance`), **motion-meaning** (every animation expresses cause-effect, never decoration), **reduced-motion** fully honoured (per `reduced-motion`). The Motion library carries: a hero with character-by-character entrance under `spring-physics`; a marquee of services running across one band like an exam-room equipment label belt with `pause-on-hover`; a sticky-scroll chief-veterinarian biography; **three-dimensional tilt** on service cards that mimics a clipboard lifted from the counter; a **magnetic CTA** at the booking moment; a counter that fills as the eye reaches the thirty-year stat; line-by-line manifesto reveal timed to a slow reading pace, not a marketing-attention pace. The page interrupts cleanly under user input (per `interruptible`) and never blocks tap during transition (per `no-blocking-animation`).

Composition is asymmetric, ruled, and generous. Where a chain animal hospital site would print *"We love your pets!"* with a stock photo of a golden retriever wearing a stethoscope, this one prints *"Thirty years. Two surgical theatres. One head veterinarian since 1995."* in deep sepia EB Garamond and lets the specifics replace the sentiment. Where another would say *"Compassionate care!"*, this one publishes the head vet's name and surgical specialisation, a complete service price-list in tabular sums, and an honest hours panel that names — and fixes — the weekday-only constraint that has cost the practice weekend-emergency revenue for years. Specifics replace adjectives. Numbers replace exclamation marks. The page is what a thirty-year clinic should have been printing for thirty years.
